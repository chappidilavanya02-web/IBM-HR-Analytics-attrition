# What's Driving Our Employee Turnover? (An Internal HR Analysis)

We built an internal analytics system to figure out exactly why people are resigning from our company, and more importantly, how we can spot a flight risk before they hand in their notice. 

To make sure this tool is actually useful for our HR team, we designed it with a strict rule: **Zero false alarms**. The system will only flag an employee as a retention risk if it is **85% or more certain** that they are planning to leave. This keeps us from wasting time chasing ghosts or micromanaging happy employees. When a name pops up, it means a real conversation needs to happen.

---

## 5 Big Stories Hidden in Our Data

*   **The Sales department is under serious pressure.** Sales is currently our most unstable department, losing more than 20% of its entire workforce. For context, our R&D and engineering teams are highly stable and staying put. 
*   **It’s specifically a "Sales Executive" problem.** The turnover in Sales isn't random. Sales Executives alone account for nearly half (45%) of all company resignations. Whatever quota pressure, travel fatigue, or stress they are experiencing, it’s actively pushing them out the door.
*   **We have a $2,000 baseline pay gap.** Money is a massive trigger here. On average, the employees who choose to stay with us make $6,833 a month, while the ones who quit were averaging $4,787. People are clearly jumping ship to get a market-rate payday elsewhere.
*   **Burnout isn't just a buzzword; it's a metric.** Out of everyone who rated their work-life balance as "Poor," nearly 1 out of 3 walked out. The good news? Simply moving people up to a fair, decent balance cuts that quitting rate exactly in half.
*   **The "3-Year Itch" is real.** Employees who stay with us generally hit a comfortable groove around Year 6. But for the people who resign, their median lifespan here is exactly 3 years. The 36-month mark is a massive mental wall where people reassess their future with us.

---

## The Top 5 Triggers For Quitting
When the system evaluates an employee profile, these are the 5 biggest factors it looks at to determine if someone is a risk:

1. **The Paycheck:** Am I being underpaid compared to the company average?
2. **The Overtime:** Am I consistently drowning in extra hours?
3. **The Commute:** Is a long, grueling daily drive wearing me down?
4. **The Glass Ceiling:** Have I been stuck in the exact same job role for years without a promotion?
5. **The Manager:** Have I been under the same direct supervisor for too long without a change of pace?

---

## What We Need to Do Next

*   **Act fast on high-risk alerts:** When the 85% filter flags an employee, treat it as a priority signal. Schedule a casual check-in or a "stay interview" immediately to see what we can fix.
*   **Intercept at the 2.5-Year mark:** Don't wait until Year 3 to see how people are doing. Set up automated career reviews at the 24-to-30-month milestone to explicitly map out their promotion path and look at their compensation.
*   **Audit the Sales Executive role:** Sit down with Sales leadership to figure out how to bridge that $2,000 compensation gap and put some boundaries around mandatory overtime before we lose more of the team.
